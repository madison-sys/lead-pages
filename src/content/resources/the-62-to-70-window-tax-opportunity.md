Most pre-retirees spend 30 years building retirement savings and then hand a third of it to the IRS — not because they had to, but because they didn't know about the window they had to act.

## Who This Is For

Americans in their late 50s and early 60s with $500K or more in traditional retirement accounts who are within 1–10 years of retirement.

This guide is especially useful if you've heard about Roth conversions but aren't sure how they interact with Social Security timing, Medicare premiums, and capital gains planning. The moves covered here are only available during a narrow window — and most people find out about them too late to act.

## The Window That Most People Miss

Once Social Security starts and RMDs kick in at 73, your income is largely set for the rest of your life. The IRS will tell you how much to take from your accounts each year — and tax you on it at whatever rate applies.

But between the day you retire and the day those distributions become mandatory, you have a window where your taxable income is lower than it's been in decades. No W-2. No required distributions. Potentially no Social Security income yet.

That's the 62-to-70 window. And the strategies inside this guide only work while it's open.
